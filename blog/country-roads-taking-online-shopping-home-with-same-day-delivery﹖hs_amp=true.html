Country roads: Taking online shopping home with same-day delivery

Written by Will Walker | Jan 5, 2026 12:00:01 PM

From coast to coast, a strategic network of retail fulfillment centers helps ensure that when online shoppers click “buy now,” there’s a high likelihood that they can get their online order within a couple of hours. More and more, online retailers are offering same-day delivery options to help meet rising customer expectations, like the 80% of consumers who expect same-day delivery when they shop online. 

But for rural shoppers, it’s a different story. Take Texas, for example. Everything’s bigger in Texas — including the distance between those major metro areas. Seymour, Texas, for instance, sits more than 50 miles from Wichita Falls, 100 miles from Abilene, and more than 150 miles from Dallas. They don’t have a superstore right up the road. As a result, like rural residents everywhere, Seymour’s 2,500 shoppers have a different perspective on what “fast delivery” really means. 

But with that in mind, more retailers are coming to understand that rural customers deserve the same delivery speed and convenience as their urban and suburban counterparts. Understanding how these consumers shop online is key, and so is offering a great delivery experience, even if it’s off the beaten path.

Rural Shopper Snapshot

A recent Roadie consumer survey reveals key differences in rural, suburban and urban customers’ delivery priorities. For the survey, urban shoppers live within a major metro area, suburban shoppers are within 50 miles of those areas, and rural customers live outside that 50-mile radius. Here are the most telling highlights:

1. Delivery Windows Matter Less

While most online shoppers prefer to choose a delivery window for expensive or oversized items, this preference is less critical in rural areas.

    1. For high-value items ($200 and over), only 47% of rural residents called delivery window selection “extremely important,” compared to 64% of urban and 63% of suburban shoppers.
    2. For oversized items, 42% of rural shoppers considered delivery windows extremely important, compared to 55% of urban and 52% of suburban residents.

Privacy and proximity likely play a role in this difference. Twenty percent of urban dwellers report having had items stolen from their doorsteps while they were away from home, but only 9% of rural customers say the same. 

2. Rural Customers Expect More Delivery Problems

Rural residents are well aware of the issues their far-flung locations can bring, and they’re twice as likely to expect one bad delivery to lead to more. When asked how they’d react to a poor delivery experience, 16% of rural shoppers said they would expect future orders to also be problematic, compared to 11% of urban and 8% of suburban respondents.

Additionally, rural shoppers are least likely to sign up for memberships or loyalty programs that include free delivery as a benefit (23% compared to 32% for urban and 29% for suburban shoppers). This hesitancy could hint at skepticism among rural residents: Why should I pay for a perk if I don’t expect the delivery to go smoothly?

3. Who Takes the Blame?

When delivery problems do arise, rural shoppers are more inclined to place the blame on the delivery company than on the retailer. 

    1. 28% of rural shoppers said the delivery company is most responsible for a negative delivery experience (compared to 20% for both urban and suburban customers).
    2. By contrast, 47% of suburban shoppers would split the blame between retailers and delivery companies, compared to 38% of rural customers.

That’s not to say retailers are in the clear if a delivery experience goes wrong. Damaged items and orders never received are among the top delivery incidents that would deter them from shopping with that retailer again. Among rural respondents, 44% say not receiving an order would drive them to shop with another retailer next time. 

Ultimately, retailers need to pay close attention to the delivery partner they select for rural areas. Knowing that rural customers are already primed to expect something will go wrong, and they’re willing to shop elsewhere if the infraction is bad enough, delivery performance will strongly shape brand perception in rural areas.
